Title: JDCNet: Confidence-Gated Privileged-Modality Distillation for
       Cost-Preserving X-ray Inference

Submission category: Original Research Article

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We submit for your consideration the manuscript entitled "JDCNet:
Confidence-Gated Privileged-Modality Distillation for Cost-Preserving X-ray
Inference" as an Original Research Article for Computerized Medical Imaging
and Graphics.

WHAT THE PAPER STUDIES

We investigate whether computed tomography (CT), available during model
development, can improve an X-ray-only classifier at deployment without
inflating its inference cost. We propose JDCNet, a confidence-gated
distillation framework in which a frozen CT teacher supplies an auxiliary
training target only on samples it classifies confidently; at test time the
deployed model is a standard single-modality X-ray classifier identical in
parameters, MACs, and latency to the supervised baseline.

MAIN FINDINGS

On a 510-patient same-patient paired BIMCV cohort evaluated under patient-level
5-fold cross-validation, two JDCNet configurations pass a pre-specified transfer
gate against the matched supervised ResNet-18 baseline:

  - 3-slice CT teacher with soft-KL target:   Delta BA = +0.035,
                                               95% CI [+0.011, +0.057]
  - Mid-slice CT teacher with hard target:     Delta BA = +0.033,
                                               95% CI [+0.007, +0.058]

Under the same protocol and gate, seven alternative transfer mechanisms --
including gated logit distillation, contrastive alignment, attention transfer,
feature hints, and BiomedCLIP fine-tuning -- do not pass. The gate decision is
statistically insensitive to teacher confidence calibration, and on an
independent external cohort both the supervised and JDCNet students fall to
chance (BA ~0.49), bounding the validated claim to the BIMCV cohort and
motivating external paired-cohort replication.

RELEVANCE TO COMPUTERIZED MEDICAL IMAGING AND GRAPHICS

The paper addresses a problem of direct relevance to computational medical
imaging: many hospitals acquire CT and X-ray for the same patient during
diagnosis but face practical constraints that favour X-ray-only automated
screening at deployment. The same training-only privileged-modality pattern
recurs across radiology (CT to X-ray), pathology (whole-slide imaging to
cytology), and ophthalmology (OCT to fundus photography). Beyond the specific
method, the paper contributes a rigorous evidence-bounded evaluation protocol
-- pre-specified gate criterion, patient-level evaluation unit, and explicit
reporting of failed comparator channels -- that we believe sets a useful
standard for small paired-cohort medical AI studies.
