\section*{Manuscript}
\textbf{Title:} Evidence-Bounded Evaluation of Cross-Modal CT-to-X-ray Knowledge Distillation under Paired-Cohort Constraints

\section*{Overall Recommendation}
\textbf{Recommendation: Reject in the current form, but encourage a substantially revised resubmission.}

The manuscript addresses an interesting and practically meaningful question: whether CT can serve as training-only privileged supervision for an X-ray-only deployed classifier under same-patient paired-cohort constraints. The paper is commendably cautious in its interpretation and provides extensive negative-result evidence, repeated patient-level resampling, imbalance-sensitive analysis, and reproducibility material. However, in its current form, the work is not yet suitable for IEEE Transactions on Circuits and Systems for Video Technology (TCSVT).

The main reasons are: (i) the manuscript does not comply with TCSVT length and abstract requirements; (ii) the connection to the circuits-and-systems/video-technology scope is still weak; (iii) the primary paired cohort is extremely small and cannot support a strong journal-level technical claim; (iv) the proposed module stack is empirically not validated; and (v) the paper reads more like an exhaustive audit report or negative-result technical report than a focused TCSVT article with a clearly established technical contribution.

A revised version could become publishable if it is reframed as a concise evidence-bounded visual-systems audit paper, reduced to the TCSVT page limit, strengthened with a clearer systems contribution, and supported by a cleaner primary experimental protocol.

\section*{Reviewer Confidence}
\textbf{Confidence: 4/5.}

The review is based on the full manuscript, including the main text, appendix, experimental tables, and the stated TCSVT submission requirements.

\section*{Summary of the Paper}
The paper studies cross-modal knowledge distillation from CT to chest X-ray. The teacher uses CT during training, while the deployed student uses only X-ray at inference. The paper compares student-only X-ray learning, late fusion, same-modality distillation, plain cross-modal logit KD, attention transfer, feature hints, generic KD baselines, and a module-augmented variant referred to as Full JDCNet with DPE/MHRA/DFPN.

The main result is largely negative. Plain cross-modal logit KD and attention transfer show the strongest directional behavior in the primary ten-resample paired-cohort experiment, but their paired advantage over supervised X-ray is not statistically significant. Full JDCNet does not improve over the stripped-down plain KD baseline. Larger BIMCV, MIDRC, DRR-teacher, and calibration follow-ups also fail to establish a statistically reliable cross-modal transfer advantage. The manuscript therefore positions itself as an evidence-bounded audit protocol and negative-result benchmark rather than a validated architecture paper.

\section*{Strengths}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{S\arabic*.},leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Important practical question.} The training-only CT supervision setting is practically meaningful because many deployment environments can only acquire or process X-ray at inference time.

    \item \textbf{Conservative and honest interpretation.} The paper does not overclaim a positive result. It clearly states that cross-modal KD is not statistically superior to supervised X-ray under the available paired-cohort scale.

    \item \textbf{Good emphasis on patient-level evaluation.} The use of patient-level splits and same-case repeated resampling is appropriate and much stronger than image-level random splitting.

    \item \textbf{Useful negative-result evidence.} The finding that added module complexity does not help is valuable, especially because many cross-modal distillation papers overstate architectural gains on small datasets.

    \item \textbf{Reproducibility effort.} The release of manifests, repeated-run summaries, and a Code Ocean capsule is a strong point and aligns well with modern reproducibility expectations.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Major Concerns}

\subsection*{M1. The manuscript is far beyond the TCSVT page limit}
The current PDF is approximately 31 pages. TCSVT Transactions Paper submissions are expected to be much shorter. The manuscript should be reduced to a focused 12--14 page journal article. The current appendix contains many post-hoc experiments, tables, and diagnostic analyses that should be moved to supplementary material or to the Code Ocean capsule.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Reduce the main manuscript to the TCSVT page limit.
    \item Keep only the essential main evidence: problem formulation, primary paired-cohort results, BIMCV/MIDRC summary, deployment efficiency, and final negative-result conclusion.
    \item Move detailed per-seed results, calibration scans, threshold sweeps, DRR pilots, MIDRC fold tables, and power analyses to supplementary material.
    \item Replace long narrative appendix sections with a compact reproducibility table and external capsule instructions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M2. The TCSVT scope fit is not yet convincing}
TCSVT is not primarily a medical imaging journal. The paper must more clearly explain its contribution to visual systems, efficient image/video understanding, and deployment-oriented inference. Currently, much of the manuscript reads as a medical AI evaluation paper. The visual-systems angle is mentioned, but it is not developed enough.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Reframe the paper around \emph{training-only privileged modality learning for efficient visual deployment}.
    \item Emphasize the X-ray-only inference path, latency, model size, and deployment constraints as central systems contributions.
    \item Clarify why this is relevant to TCSVT beyond the medical application.
    \item Add comparisons or discussion with TCSVT-relevant works on efficient visual recognition, cross-modal visual learning, multimodal compression/distillation, and deployment-oriented image/video analysis.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M3. The abstract is too long and too detailed}
The abstract is over the recommended length and contains many abbreviations and detailed numerical claims. It currently reads like a compressed results table rather than a concise journal abstract.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Shorten the abstract to 150--200 words, define only essential terms, and avoid excessive acronyms such as DPE/MHRA/DFPN, DRR, and Path-C unless they are central to the paper.

\textbf{Suggested revised abstract:}
\begin{quote}
We study whether chest computed tomography (CT) can provide training-only privileged supervision for an X-ray-only classifier under same-patient paired-cohort constraints. The goal is to test whether a richer auxiliary modality can improve a lightweight deployment path without requiring CT at inference. We compare supervised X-ray learning, late fusion, same-modality distillation, cross-modal logit distillation, attention transfer, feature-hint transfer, generic knowledge-distillation baselines, and a module-augmented variant. Under patient-level repeated resampling, cross-modal logit distillation and attention transfer show the strongest directional behavior, but their advantage over supervised X-ray is not statistically significant. The module-augmented variant underperforms the stripped-down control, and larger BIMCV/MIDRC stress tests do not establish a reliable cross-modal gain. These results do not validate a new architecture. Instead, they provide a reproducible evidence-bounded audit protocol showing that, at the current paired-cohort scale, CT-to-X-ray distillation remains effectively tied with supervised X-ray learning. The study highlights the need for larger same-patient cohorts and stronger transfer mechanisms before positive cross-modal claims can be considered reliable.
\end{quote}

\subsection*{M4. The technical contribution is weakened by the negative architecture result}
The paper introduces or discusses Full JDCNet with DPE, MHRA, and DFPN, but the experiments show that the module stack does not improve over plain cross-modal logit KD. This creates a mismatch between architectural naming and empirical conclusion. A paper titled or framed around JDCNet would be difficult to accept because the architecture is not validated.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Avoid presenting JDCNet as the central contribution.
    \item Rename Full JDCNet as a \emph{module-augmented stress-test variant}.
    \item Make the audit protocol, not the failed architecture, the primary contribution.
    \item If the authors want to keep JDCNet as a contribution, they must provide a revised architecture that consistently improves over plain logit KD on a sufficiently powered cohort.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M5. The primary paired cohort is too small for a strong journal-level claim}
The paired X-ray target cohort contains only 26 images from 19 patients, with only four negative patients. Even repeated resampling cannot fully solve this limitation because each validation split contains only one negative case. The paper appropriately acknowledges this, but the amount of analysis performed on this tiny cohort is disproportionate to its evidential strength.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Clearly distinguish between feasibility evidence, primary evidence, and post-hoc stress tests.
    \item Avoid reporting too many decimal-level metrics on tiny validation splits.
    \item Provide exact patient IDs or anonymized split indices for every resample in the reproducibility package.
    \item Use the larger BIMCV/MIDRC results as the main support if they are intended to carry the paper.
    \item Alternatively, explicitly position the original paired cohort only as a motivating pilot.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M6. The statistical analysis still risks pseudo-precision}
The paper uses repeated resampling, Wilcoxon tests, bootstrap CIs, and many post-hoc comparisons. However, because the same small patient pool is repeatedly reused, these resamples are not equivalent to independent validation cohorts. Some p-values and confidence intervals may therefore give an impression of greater precision than the data support.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Add a short statistical caveat explaining that resamples are correlated because they reuse the same patient pool.
    \item Prefer hierarchical or patient-level bootstrap/permutation analysis where possible.
    \item Correct or clearly mark multiple-comparison tests as exploratory.
    \item Report effect sizes and win/loss counts alongside p-values.
    \item Avoid interpreting uncorrected p-values against generic KD baselines as strong evidence.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M7. The paper contains too many post-hoc experiments}
The appendix contains BIMCV stress tests, Path-C re-splits, GAP-KD follow-ups, MIDRC rounds, same-source BIMCV CV, calibration scans, DRR pilots, and power analysis. These are useful, but the presentation is too sprawling and makes the core message difficult to follow.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Define one primary endpoint before presenting results.
    \item Group all post-hoc analyses into one compact ``stress-test summary'' table.
    \item Move detailed post-hoc narratives to supplementary material.
    \item State explicitly which experiments were pre-specified and which were exploratory.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M8. Baseline selection should be strengthened and simplified}
The paper includes many KD baselines, but the most important deployment baselines are not fully integrated into the main argument. For example, the ImageNet-pretrained ResNet18 baseline achieves perfect results on the tiny fixed split, but this result is not meaningful enough as presented. BiomedCLIP is used only as a frozen feature extractor, which may be too weak a comparison.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Use a consistent backbone family for supervised, teacher, and KD comparisons.
    \item Include stronger X-ray-only baselines trained or fine-tuned on relevant chest X-ray data.
    \item Consider foundation-model baselines with fine-tuning, not only frozen linear probing.
    \item For CT teachers, compare mid-slice, multi-slice, volume aggregation, and DRR teachers under the same protocol.
    \item Remove baselines that repeatedly collapse unless they are needed for a specific failure-mode argument.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M9. The CT representation is under-specified for a cross-modal medical claim}
The main paired experiment selects the most central axial CT slice. This is a severe simplification of CT information and may not reflect the actual disease-relevant region. If CT is claimed to be a richer teacher modality, the teacher should exploit CT more meaningfully.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Justify why a single central axial slice is sufficient.
    \item Add or emphasize multi-slice/volume teacher experiments.
    \item Report how CT-X-ray temporal offsets are handled and whether disease progression could confound pairing.
    \item Clarify whether CT and X-ray labels are patient-level, study-level, or image-level labels.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M10. Clinical validity and dataset bias remain serious limitations}
The paper uses public COVID imaging collections that are known to contain source artifacts and potential shortcut biases. The cross-source non-COVID control collapses toward all-positive prediction, which is a serious warning sign.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Make the shortcut-learning and source-bias risk more prominent in the main text.
    \item Do not imply diagnostic generalization.
    \item Add a dataset-bias paragraph near the main experimental results, not only in the appendix.
    \item If possible, add an external same-patient paired validation cohort with adequate negative support.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M11. The deployment-efficiency analysis is useful but not central enough}
For TCSVT, the deployment-time systems view should be a main contribution. The latency table is useful, but it appears late and does not drive the paper's argument.

\textbf{Required revision:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Move deployment efficiency into the main experimental narrative.
    \item Report CPU/GPU latency, MACs, parameters, memory, and throughput for all deployable student variants.
    \item Discuss the cost-benefit trade-off: Full JDCNet is more expensive but not more accurate.
    \item If possible, include an edge-device or embedded-platform measurement, which would strengthen the TCSVT systems fit.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{M12. The paper's main conclusion should be sharper}
The paper is honest but sometimes too verbose. The central conclusion should be stated more directly: the study does not validate CT-to-X-ray KD as superior to supervised X-ray at the current evidence scale.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Use a sharper conclusion:
\begin{quote}
At the available paired-cohort scale, CT teachers are feasible but do not yield a statistically reliable improvement over supervised X-ray learning. Added transfer modules increase complexity without improving the deployed student. The main contribution is therefore an evidence-bounded audit protocol and negative-result benchmark, not a validated cross-modal architecture.
\end{quote}

\section*{Minor Concerns}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{m\arabic*.},leftmargin=*]
    \item Several abbreviations are introduced too early or used without sufficient motivation, including DPE, MHRA, DFPN, DRR, GAP-KD, and Path-C.
    \item The title is accurate but long. A shorter title would improve readability.
    \item The term ``JDCNet'' should either be removed or clearly described as a legacy/internal label.
    \item Some figures are too small for IEEE two-column format. Figure labels and legends should be enlarged.
    \item Many tables contain too much information. Main-text tables should be reduced to the minimum necessary evidence.
    \item The paper alternates between ``artifact'' and ``artefact.'' Use one spelling consistently.
    \item Institution names should be checked for IEEE style, e.g., ``Guilin'' rather than ``Gui Lin.''
    \item References should be checked for complete IEEE style, DOI information where available, and final publication venues rather than arXiv-only entries where possible.
    \item The Code Ocean capsule is a strength, but the paper should include exact reproduction commands.
    \item The ethics statement should also mention dataset licenses and redistribution restrictions.
    \item The abstract should not include excessive numerical details that are better placed in the Results section.
    \item The contribution bullets should be reduced from broad claims to three concise, defensible claims.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Suggested Restructuring for a TCSVT Resubmission}

\subsection*{Recommended New Structure}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Introduction}:
    Define the problem as training-only privileged modality learning for efficient visual deployment. Explain why this is a TCSVT visual-systems problem.

    \item \textbf{Related Work}:
    Cover cross-modal distillation, privileged information, efficient visual inference, medical image learning, and evidence robustness.

    \item \textbf{Evidence-Bounded Protocol}:
    Present the patient-level paired evaluation protocol, split rules, metrics, and statistical criteria.

    \item \textbf{Methods}:
    Describe the teacher-student setup, plain logit KD, attention transfer, and the optional module-augmented variant. Keep this concise.

    \item \textbf{Experiments}:
    Present only the main cohort, the larger BIMCV/MIDRC stress summary, and deployment efficiency.

    \item \textbf{Discussion}:
    Explain why the result is negative, what this implies for future paired CT/CXR studies, and what evidence scale is needed next.

    \item \textbf{Conclusion}:
    State clearly that the contribution is an audit protocol and negative-result benchmark.
\end{enumerate}

Evidence-Bounded Audit of Training-Only CT Supervision for X-ray-Only Visual Inference
\end{quote}

\section*{Confidential Comments to the Editor}
This manuscript is unusually honest and reproducible, and its negative-result framing is scientifically useful. However, it is not ready for TCSVT in its present form. The manuscript is far over the journal's page limit, its TCSVT systems contribution is underdeveloped, and the central architecture is not empirically validated. The paper may be valuable after major restructuring as a concise evidence-bounded audit of training-only privileged modality learning for efficient visual inference. I recommend rejection in the current form, with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revision.

\section*{Final Recommendation}
\textbf{Reject / Resubmit after major restructuring.}

The work has potential, but acceptance would require: formal TCSVT compliance, a much shorter and more focused manuscript, stronger systems framing, clearer statistical treatment, and a revised contribution statement that honestly centers the negative-result audit rather than an unsupported architecture claim.
