\section*{Overall Evaluation}

The manuscript now reads as a coherent, evidence-bounded pilot study rather than as an over-claimed new-method paper. The strongest improvements from the previous cycle are now in place: the problem formulation is stable, the fixed split and repeated same-case resampling are clearly separated by evidential role, the method section is less architecture-heavy, and the main paper now signals support counts and regime roles much more explicitly. The current draft is therefore materially closer to a credible MICCAI-style pilot or reproducibility submission than it was one revision round ago.

At this stage, the remaining issues are no longer about scientific overclaim in the core experimental logic. They are about \textbf{final positioning discipline}. The draft still presents itself a little too much like a named-method paper in a few high-visibility places, and one headline results table still gives slightly too much visual weight to a reference-only CT row that is not genuinely comparable to the paired-cohort evidence. In addition, the first related-work subsection can still be upgraded from ``COVID-specific prior work'' to ``broader thoracic imaging context'' with one or two stronger anchor references.

\section*{Major Strengths of the Current Draft}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The evidential hierarchy is now credible.} The paper no longer treats the four-image paired split as if it were a definitive benchmark result. The fixed split is framed as feasibility-only, and repeated same-case resampling is treated as the primary evidence.
    \item \textbf{The manuscript now communicates failure modes honestly.} The plain cross-modal logit-KD control, same-modality KD, late fusion, and the full JDCNet scaffold are all discussed in a way that matches the observed instability instead of masking it.
    \item \textbf{The method section is much better aligned with the actual claim boundary.} Section 3 now explains what is tested without over-selling DPE, MHRA, and DFPN as already-validated contributions.
    \item \textbf{Support counts are now substantially harder to miss.} The evaluation-regime summary and the revised captions reduce the risk that a reviewer skimming tables and figures will overestimate the strength of the paired-cohort evidence.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Priority 1: De-emphasize Named-Method Branding in the Highest-Visibility Locations}

\textbf{Current issue:}

Although the body of the paper now reads like a pilot study, the title and a few other highly visible locations still foreground the named system ``JDCNet'' more strongly than the actual evidential contribution. This creates a subtle mismatch: the paper's strongest claim is not that JDCNet is a validated architecture, but that the manuscript provides a reproducible and evidence-bounded study of CT-to-X-ray transfer under tiny paired cohorts.

\textbf{Revision action:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Consider retitling the paper so that the task and evidential framing appear before the method name, or remove the method name from the title entirely.
    \item Apply the same positioning rule to the appendix title, PDF metadata, and first method-facing sentence of the abstract.
    \item Keep ``JDCNet'' as the name of the executable scaffold inside the manuscript, but avoid letting the title make the paper read like a classic new-architecture submission.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Priority 2: Demote the CT-Only Reference Out of the Headline Fixed-Split Table}

\textbf{Current issue:}

The fixed-split table is much more interpretable than before, but the CT-only row is still a reference-only result with no negative validation patient and no meaningful same-case comparability. Even with improved labeling, it still competes visually with the paired-cohort rows in the main results table.

\textbf{Revision action:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Move the CT-only reference row out of the headline fixed-split table and into the appendix, or into a short textual note immediately below the table.
    \item Keep the all-X-ray reference row in the main table if desired, because it remains the most interpretable large-cohort anchor.
    \item Adjust the corresponding paragraph in the fixed-split subsection so that the main text discusses only rows that materially support the paper's argument.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Priority 3: Strengthen the First Related-Work Subsection with Broader Thoracic Imaging Anchors}

\textbf{Current issue:}

The privileged-information, supervision-transfer, and evidence-robustness lines are now much better anchored. The weakest part of Related Work is instead the opening subsection on chest X-ray and CT classification, which still leans heavily on a narrow set of COVID-specific model papers. For a MICCAI-facing reader, that subsection would benefit from one or two broader thoracic-imaging anchors, such as a respected survey or a widely recognized chest X-ray benchmark paper.

\textbf{Revision action:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Add one stronger chest X-ray anchor and, if space permits, one broader thoracic or medical-imaging survey anchor.
    \item Use those citations to make the opening related-work subsection less dataset-specific and less tied to early-pandemic model literature.
    \item Do not expand the whole literature section again; this is a targeted anchoring fix, not a call for another broad literature pass.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Priority 4: Trim the Fixed-Split Narrative Slightly Now That the Tables Carry More of the Load}

\textbf{Current issue:}

The main paper now has a solid evidential legend and stronger captions. Because of that, the fixed-split narrative can probably be shortened by a few sentences without losing interpretability. The goal is not to remove the cautionary explanation, but to let the table, figure, and resampling section carry more of the explanatory burden.

\textbf{Revision action:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Remove one or two sentences from the fixed-split subsection that restate points already explicit in the revised table caption and figure caption.
    \item Preserve the sentence that explains why the plain baseline is still unstable and why the CT-only row is not a valid headline comparator.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Non-Priority Guidance}

    \item Do not re-expand Section 3.
    \item Do not broaden the claim beyond ``training-only CT supervision for X-ray deployment under tiny patient-level paired cohorts''.
\end{enumerate}
