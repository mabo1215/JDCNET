\section*{Summary of Submission}
The manuscript investigates whether chest computed tomography (CT) can serve
as a training-only privileged-information signal for an X-ray-only deployed
binary COVID-19 classifier. The authors deliberately frame the paper as a
``falsification-oriented'' pilot study: they design an eleven-method
comparison matrix (student-only, late fusion, same-modality KD, plain cross-modal
logit KD, attention transfer, feature hint, Full JDCNet with three optional
modules DPE/MHRA/DFPN, plus four published KD baselines--CRD, DKD, DIST,
modality-hallucination KD), and evaluate them under (i) a fixed-split
feasibility screen on a 4-image paired validation cohort and (ii) a primary
ten-resample patient-level Monte Carlo protocol. They report that plain
cross-modal logit KD and attention transfer attain the highest mean balanced
accuracy ($0.900\pm0.211$, $+0.100$ over the X-ray-only baseline, Wilcoxon
$p=0.625$, $n=10$), significantly outperform DKD/DIST/MH ($p=0.031$) and
directionally outperform CRD ($p=0.063$), while the full JDCNet module stack
reaches only $0.750$ and is therefore \emph{not} validated. A 512-patient
BIMCV paired stress test and several follow-up executions (ResNet-18 backbone,
prototype-augmented KD, DRR-teacher sweep, protocol correction, balanced
Path-C re-split) are reported in the appendix and consistently fail to
produce statistically significant cross-modal gains.

\section*{Overall Recommendation}
\textbf{Reject (with strong encouragement to resubmit elsewhere, or to redirect
to a venue more aligned with the contribution).}

\medskip
The manuscript is unusually transparent and methodologically self-aware---the
authors themselves repeatedly state that no claim reaches statistical
significance and that the proposed modules are not validated. As a piece of
\emph{evidence-bounded reporting} it is admirable. However, TCSVT publishes
``general, theoretical, and application-oriented papers with a circuits and
systems perspective \ldots\ on or related to image/video acquisition,
representation, presentation and display; processing, filtering and
transforms; analysis and synthesis; learning and understanding; \ldots''.
Measured against that scope and the journal's expectation that submissions
report ``important research results,'' the present manuscript faces two
fundamental problems: (1) a \textbf{scope mismatch}---it is a medical-imaging
pilot study with no circuits/systems contribution, no video component, and
no architectural innovation that has been validated; (2) a \textbf{null-result
problem}---every primary hypothesis (H1--H4) is either ``directional, not
significant'' or ``not supported,'' and the proposed JDCNet architecture is
explicitly shown to be worse than the simplest control. There is no positive
methodological or empirical claim that survives the authors' own evaluation
protocol.

Detailed comments follow.

\section{Major Issues}

\subsection{No positive validated claim}
Table 2 (the authors' own ``claim-status summary'') tells the reader on
page 3 that:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item H1 (cross-modal KD $>$ student-only): \emph{directional, not significant} ($p=0.625$);
  \item H2 (gain not reducible to same-modality KD): \emph{directional, not significant} ($p=0.250$);
  \item H3 (gain not reducible to late fusion): \emph{not supported};
  \item H4 (DPE/MHRA/DFPN improve over plain control): \emph{not supported};
  \item H5 (gain survives imbalance/threshold checks): only \emph{partially supported}.
\end{itemize}
In other words, the authors themselves declare that none of the five
hypotheses around which the paper is organised reach significance. The
single significant finding---that plain logit KD/AT outperforms DKD, DIST,
and MH at $p=0.031$---rests on $n=10$ resamples drawn from a paired cohort
with \textbf{only four COVID-negative patients in total} and \emph{one
negative validation patient per resample}. Sign-test power at $n=10$ with
binary outcomes is so low that the reported $p=0.031$ is a near-floor value
of the test, not evidence of a robust effect.

The appendix's BIMCV stress test (Executions A--F, Table 23) confirms this:
on a 512-patient cohort and a corrected 33-patient holdout protocol, plain
CT logit KD gives $\Delta=+0.014$, two-sided $p=0.106$, and the
balanced-validation Path-C re-split reduces $\Delta$ to $+0.002$ (essentially
tied). \emph{Every} variant (prototype-KD, DRR-KD, DRR-KD+AT,
DRR-KD+AT+Proto) either fails to reach significance or is significantly
\emph{worse} than supervised. The honest conclusion the manuscript reaches is
that ``CT-to-X-ray distillation does not work at currently achievable
paired-cohort scales.'' That is a legitimate negative result, but it is not
a contribution that meets the bar of a TCSVT regular paper.

\subsection{The architectural contribution is explicitly falsified by the authors}
JDCNet is presented as the named architecture in the title and Figure 1, yet
Section 4.5 and Table 6 show that the full module stack
(DPE+MHRA+DFPN) achieves $0.750$ balanced accuracy---\emph{lower} than the
stripped-down plain logit KD control ($0.900$) and lower than the X-ray-only
supervised baseline ($0.800$). The progressive-complexity experiment in
Table 21 shows balanced accuracy degrades monotonically as modules are added:
$0.750 \to 0.625 \to 0.625 \to 0.500 \to 0.500$. The authors describe DPE,
MHRA, and DFPN as ``optional, auditable complexity increments'' rather than
validated contributions. \textbf{A paper whose named architecture is, by the
authors' own demonstration, strictly dominated by a much simpler baseline
cannot reasonably be published under that architecture's name.} If the
authors wish to retain the falsification-oriented framing, the paper should
be retitled and reorganised so that the \emph{protocol} (not JDCNet) is the
contribution; in its current form, the title misrepresents the actual
findings.

\subsection{Evidence scale: the cohort is too small to support any positive claim}
The paired cohort contains:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item 26 X-ray images from 19 patients (22 positive, 4 negative);
  \item a fixed validation split of \textbf{4 images / 4 patients (3 positive, 1 negative)};
  \item 10 resamples, each with \textbf{1 negative validation patient by construction}.
\end{itemize}
With $N_{\text{neg}}=4$ \emph{in the entire cohort}, the specificity in any
resample is a single Bernoulli draw, and the SD column for specificity-bound
metrics in Tables 6 and 13 (reported as $\pm0.422$ to $\pm0.527$) is
mechanically the binomial spread of one negative example---it carries no
patient-level uncertainty information about method differences. The authors
acknowledge this in Appendix~A.7. The deeper problem is not that the SD
column is misleading, but that \textbf{the cohort cannot in principle support
any claim about specificity-sensitive metrics}, including balanced accuracy
and MCC. Reporting median + IQR + bootstrap CI (Table~15) does not fix this;
the bootstrap inherits the same degeneracy. The honest conclusion is that
the cohort is below the minimum size at which the chosen evaluation question
is answerable. The power analysis in Table~25 (Appendix A.21) makes this
precise: at $n_{\text{val}}=5$, detecting any effect below
$P(\Delta>0)\approx0.92$ at 80\% power is impossible.

\subsection{Dataset choice and the COVID-19 Image Data Collection caveat}
The primary dataset (Cohen et~al., COVID-19 Image Data Collection) is
explicitly cautioned by its own curators as ``not a competition benchmark
and not a substitute for clinical validation.'' The authors cite this
caveat (Section 4.9), but the manuscript still uses the dataset as the
primary evidence base for an eleven-method ranking. This is internally
inconsistent: if the dataset cannot support a ranking claim, then no amount
of resampling on top of it can. Furthermore, the published literature has
documented severe \emph{shortcut learning} (DeGrave et~al.\ 2021, [6] in the
manuscript) on exactly this collection---models latch onto site-specific
acquisition artefacts rather than COVID-19 disease patterns. The
category-level cross-source control reported in Appendix A.20 (Table 24)
confirms this: every method reaches 100\% sensitivity and near-zero
specificity on out-of-source negatives. \emph{This is a falsification of the
training signal itself, not just of the cross-modal transfer hypothesis.}
The manuscript correctly reports the result but does not adequately
foreground it: the entire downstream comparison matrix may be measuring
acquisition-artefact discrimination rather than disease classification.

\subsection{Mismatch between deployment-time framing and the actual contribution}
The paper repeatedly invokes a ``deployment-realistic X-ray-only'' framing
(Section 1.1, Table 1, Section 4.8) and reports CPU/GPU latency in
Table~8. But this framing is undermined by three observations:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The deployed student is only $0.094$M parameters and runs in
        $\sim$15~ms on CPU; this is already well below clinical-deployment
        latency floors. Adding DPE+MHRA+DFPN increases CPU latency by
        $2.1\times$ but the empirical signal worsens, so the deployment
        framing argues \emph{against} the proposed architecture rather than
        for it.
  \item No real-world deployment context, regulatory consideration, or
        clinical workflow integration is discussed. The deployment claim is
        rhetorical, not engineered.
  \item For a TCSVT audience, the natural deployment angle would be
        \emph{hardware-aware design}, quantisation, or
        compression-distillation co-design, none of which appear here.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The ``falsification-oriented protocol'' is not novel as a contribution}
The authors frame the patient-level Monte Carlo resampling with same-case
evaluation, balance-sensitive metric reporting, and prevalence-aware ranking
as their primary methodological contribution. While the bundle is
\emph{well-executed}, none of its components is new:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Patient-level cross-validation in medical imaging is standard
        practice (cited in Maier-Hein et~al.\ 2018, Roberts et~al.\ 2021).
  \item Balanced accuracy, MCC, and PR-AUC are routine.
  \item Monte Carlo resampling and bootstrap CIs are textbook.
  \item Same-case (paired) comparison via Wilcoxon signed-rank is the
        default test in small-cohort medical-imaging papers.
\end{itemize}
The contribution is therefore the \emph{application} of standard tools to a
particular tiny cohort, not a new evaluation methodology. The CLAIM-style
checklist (Table 9) and the Code Ocean capsule are valuable for
reproducibility, but they are also standard expectations now, not novel
contributions.

\subsection{Method section lacks engineering depth expected by TCSVT}
Section 3 introduces three modules (DPE, MHRA, DFPN) coined by the authors,
but the descriptions in Table 22 are one-line summaries:
\begin{quote}
\small
DPE = ``$1\times1$ convolution + spatial softmax reweighting'';\\
MHRA = ``batch-first multi-head attention + sigmoid retain gate'';\\
DFPN = ``3-scale top-down pyramid with lateral $1\times1$ and output
$3\times3$ convolutions.''
\end{quote}
These are not architectural innovations; they are off-the-shelf operators
relabelled with new acronyms. For a TCSVT submission, the reviewer expects
either (a) a genuinely new operator with mathematical analysis (gradient
behaviour, capacity, complexity bounds), or (b) a hardware/efficiency
argument tied to circuits/systems. Neither is present. The acronym coinage
(``coined by the authors; not established community names,'' Section 3.3)
should be removed; standard names should be used.

\subsection{Statistical reporting is internally inconsistent}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Table 6 reports mean$\pm$SD; Table 7 uses Wilcoxon signed-rank;
        Table 15 reports median + IQR + bootstrap CI. The text does not
        commit to which is primary. Given the binomial degeneracy of the
        specificity column, the \emph{only} defensible report is the
        median + IQR view; the mean$\pm$SD column should be removed from
        the main text or relegated to the appendix with a footnote stating
        that the SD is not a patient-level uncertainty estimate.
  \item Multiple-comparison correction is not applied. Table 7 reports
        eight pairwise Wilcoxon tests; at $\alpha=0.05$ family-wise, a
        Bonferroni correction would require $p<0.00625$, which only the
        DKD/DIST/MH comparisons pass marginally ($p=0.031$). The
        ``directional advantage over CRD'' ($p=0.063$) does not survive
        any reasonable correction.
  \item The one-sided $p=0.053$ for plain CT logit KD under the corrected
        BIMCV protocol (Section 4.10) is reported as a positive finding in
        the conclusion. With multiple methods, executions, and protocols
        tested, this is a textbook example of garden-of-forking-paths
        inference and should not be flagged as the strongest signal.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Reference list quality}
The reference list is broadly competent but has several gaps:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Cross-modal distillation has a substantial recent literature that
        is not engaged: e.g., Garcia~\&~Vidal (RGB-D action recognition),
        Thoker~\&~Gall (cross-modal action distillation), Aytar et~al.
        SoundNet, and the wave of work on cross-modal contrastive
        pretraining post-CLIP. The citation of CRD (Tian 2020) is
        appropriate but lonely.
  \item No engagement with the very large privileged-information literature
        beyond Vapnik \& Vashist (2009) and Lopez-Paz et~al.\ (2016).
  \item The KD baseline set (CRD, DKD, DIST, MH) is reasonable but stops
        at 2022; nothing post-2023 is compared. For a 2026 submission this
        is a noticeable gap.
  \item No comparison with recent vision-language models for chest imaging
        beyond a single citation of BiomedCLIP (E4 in Appendix A.22 uses
        BiomedCLIP frozen features but does not engage with the broader
        literature).
\end{itemize}

\section{Minor Issues}

\subsection{Title}
``JDCNet'' is the named architecture, but JDCNet is shown to underperform
the baseline. Either rename the paper around the protocol contribution
(e.g., ``Evidence-Bounded Evaluation of Cross-Modal CT-to-X-ray
Distillation: A Falsification Study'') or drop the JDCNet brand entirely.

\subsection{Abstract}
The abstract is unusually long ($\sim$350 words) and reads more like an
executive summary than an abstract. IEEE journals typically expect
$\sim$200 words. Tighten by removing the explicit $p$-values and the
detailed module-by-module breakdown; the abstract should state the
question, the protocol, the primary finding, and the implication.

\subsection{Figure 1 (evidence map)}
The figure is informative but its caption is long and partly
duplicates the table. Consider keeping the figure focused on the bar
chart and moving narrative interpretation to the text.

\subsection{Tables 6, 13, and 15}
Tables 6 and 13 are almost identical (main vs.\ appendix). Consolidate.

\subsection{Equation (1)}
The notation $\sigma$ is overloaded (used for both softmax and sigmoid
in different sections). Standardise.

\subsection{Equation (1) vs.\ Equations (2)--(5)}
The main-text objective and the appendix-form objective use slightly
different notation ($\alpha$ vs.\ $(1-\alpha)$ on the soft term). Make
consistent.

\subsection{Section 3.1 (Notation and Glossary)}
Move to the very beginning of Section 3 or to an appendix table; in its
current position it interrupts the methodology.

\subsection{Figure 4 (heatmap of all 0.429)}
A heatmap of nine identical values is not informative as a figure---it
is a visual representation of degenerate data. Replace with a single
sentence: ``Under the four-image fixed split, none of the nine
$(\tau, \alpha)$ combinations produced any measurable difference in
macro-F1, indicating that hyperparameter ranking is not identifiable at
this scale.''

\subsection{Figure 6 (per-seed scatter)}
The horizontal-line ``means'' overlap the dots and are visually noisy.
Consider boxplots with overlaid jittered points.

\subsection{Figures 8, 10, 13}
All three figures present essentially negative results. Consolidate into
a single ``module ablation summary'' figure with three panels.

\subsection{Section 4.8 (Deployment-Time Efficiency)}
The latency table is useful, but the row ``$+$ DPE'' showing $9.97$~ms
on CPU \emph{lower} than ``Student-only X-ray'' ($14.62$~ms) is
suspicious---adding a $1\times1$ conv plus softmax should not reduce
latency. Verify measurement setup (warm-up, batch size, single-image vs.
batched).

\subsection{Section 4.10 (Limitations and Future Work)}
This section is unusually long and reads as a partial appendix.
Restructure: pull the BIMCV stress-test narrative into the appendix
proper (where Table 23 already lives) and leave only a one-paragraph
summary in the main text.

\subsection{Appendix A.19 (Table 23 caption)}
The caption runs across two full pages and contains the central
narrative findings. Move the interpretation paragraphs into the
appendix body text; the caption should describe the table, not present
results.

\subsection{Typographical and stylistic items}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item ``Falsification-oriented'' is used $\sim$15 times; vary phrasing.
  \item ``Evidential bar / floor / boundary'' is overused.
  \item ``Tier-1/2/3'' nomenclature is introduced and then dropped
        inconsistently in tables.
  \item Several reference entries omit DOIs or proper venue capitalisation
        (e.g., [9], [10], [20]). Run BibTeX consistency pass.
  \item Some inline math uses italic ``T'' for temperature and Roman ``T''
        for transpose without distinction.
  \item Equation (1) should be labelled and referenced; it is currently
        floating.
  \item ``Hikvision Technology Co., Ltd'' appears as an affiliation for
        one author---verify that no conflict-of-interest or commercial
        disclosure is required.
  \item Patient identifiers appearing in Table 17 (e.g., ``210,'' ``65''
        plus filenames) should be verified for compliance with the
        original dataset's redistribution terms.
\end{itemize}

\section{Questions for the Authors}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Why was TCSVT chosen as the target venue rather than IEEE TMI,
        Medical Image Analysis, IEEE JBHI, or Pattern Recognition? What
        is the circuits/systems contribution the authors wish to claim?
  \item Given that the named architecture (JDCNet) is empirically
        dominated by a much simpler control, why is the paper still
        organised around it? What would change if the paper were
        retitled to centre the protocol contribution?
  \item Has the category-level cross-source collapse (Appendix A.20)
        been investigated as evidence that the training signal is
        site-specific shortcut learning rather than disease-specific?
        How does the manuscript's central claim survive if the answer
        is yes?
  \item The corrected BIMCV Path-C re-split (Execution F, Table 23)
        gives plain CT logit KD a $\Delta$ of $+0.002$ over supervised.
        Why does the conclusion still frame plain logit KD as ``the
        most stable transfer signal''? Would the authors accept that
        the honest summary is ``no validated cross-modal advantage at
        any cohort scale tested''?
  \item The progressive-complexity table (Table 21) shows monotonic
        degradation with module addition. Why are DPE, MHRA, and DFPN
        retained in the figures and abstract at all, rather than
        removed?
  \item What is the minimum same-patient paired cohort size at which
        the authors would expect their protocol to yield a definitive
        decision, and is such a cohort feasible to assemble?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Concrete Revision Suggestions (LaTeX-Ready Edits)}

If the authors choose to resubmit to a more appropriate venue (e.g.,
IEEE TMI, MIA, IEEE JBHI), the following structural revisions would
substantially strengthen the manuscript.

\subsection{Suggested revised title}
\begin{verbatim}
\title{Evidence-Bounded Evaluation of Cross-Modal CT-to-X-ray
Knowledge Distillation under Tiny Paired Cohorts:
A Falsification Study}
\end{verbatim}
This title is honest about the contribution (a protocol + a negative
finding) and removes the misleading JDCNet branding.

\subsection{Suggested revised abstract (drop-in replacement)}
\begin{verbatim}
\begin{abstract}
We study whether chest computed tomography (CT) can serve as
training-only privileged supervision for an X-ray-only deployed
binary classifier. Using a same-patient paired chest cohort and a
patient-level ten-resample Monte Carlo protocol with same-case
evaluation, we compare eleven configurations spanning student-only
training, late fusion, same-modality distillation, plain cross-modal
logit transfer, attention transfer, feature hints, four published
generic knowledge-distillation baselines (CRD, DKD, DIST,
modality-hallucination KD), and a module-augmented cross-modal
variant. Plain cross-modal logit transfer and attention transfer
achieve the highest mean balanced accuracy (0.900 +/- 0.211) but
fail to reach significance against the supervised baseline
(Wilcoxon p = 0.625, n = 10); generic KD baselines collapse to
trivial prediction on a majority of resamples; the proposed module
stack underperforms its own stripped-down control. A 512-patient
BIMCV paired stress test and four follow-up executions (richer
backbone, prototype-augmented KD, DRR-teacher anchor, balanced
validation re-split) consistently fail to deliver a statistically
significant cross-modal advantage. We conclude that cross-modal
CT-to-X-ray distillation is not validated at currently achievable
paired-cohort scales, identify the minimum next experiment, and
release a reproducible pipeline so subsequent studies can be
evaluated under a uniform evidential bar.
\end{abstract}
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Suggested reorganisation}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Section 1.} Lead with the protocol contribution; demote the
        architecture to a single sentence: ``We also evaluate three
        optional modules (DPE, MHRA, DFPN), all of which fail.''
  \item \textbf{Section 3.} Compress the method section to one page.
        Remove DPE/MHRA/DFPN from the main figure (Figure 1) and place
        them only in an appendix block diagram.
  \item \textbf{Section 4.} Restructure as: (4.1) cohort and protocol;
        (4.2) main result table (Table 6 only, with median + IQR + CI);
        (4.3) one-paragraph BIMCV stress test summary; (4.4)
        limitations.
  \item \textbf{Appendix.} Keep the existing content but split into two
        files: (A) reproducibility artefacts (manifests, CLAIM checklist,
        Code Ocean pointer); (B) negative-result audit trail (all
        execution variants, protocol corrections, calibration, power
        analysis).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Suggested statistical reporting standard}
For every method comparison, report:
\begin{verbatim}
median balanced accuracy [Q1, Q3]
95% bootstrap CI of the median (BCa where defined, percentile
   otherwise)
Wilcoxon signed-rank p-value (two-sided)
Bonferroni-corrected significance threshold for the family of
   comparisons
\end{verbatim}
Remove all $\mathrm{mean}\pm\mathrm{SD}$ reporting from the main text,
since with $n_{\text{neg}}=1$ per resample the SD column is mechanically
the binomial spread of a single Bernoulli draw.

\section{Summary of Strengths}
Despite the recommendation, the manuscript has genuine strengths that
should not be overlooked:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Methodological honesty.} The authors clearly state which
        hypotheses fail, do not over-interpret directional trends, and
        provide an unusually detailed audit trail.
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility.} The Code Ocean capsule, CLAIM
        checklist, deterministic training settings, and patient-level
        split audit are exemplary.
  \item \textbf{Negative-result transparency.} The five BIMCV executions
        (A--F), the corrected-protocol analysis, and the Path-C re-split
        document a thorough effort to test whether the negative finding
        is robust.
  \item \textbf{Cohort awareness.} The repeated acknowledgement that the
        cohort is below the minimum scale for a definitive ranking is
        unusual and welcome.
  \item \textbf{Power analysis.} Table 25 (Appendix A.21) is well-done
        and rare for medical-imaging pilots.
\end{itemize}

These strengths would be much more visible in a venue where negative
results and protocol contributions are explicitly welcomed (e.g.,
\emph{Nature Machine Intelligence}'s recent calls for negative results;
\emph{npj Digital Medicine}; or workshop tracks at MICCAI/ML4H).

\section{Final Recommendation}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Decision:} \textbf{Reject for TCSVT.}
  \item \textbf{Primary reasons:} (i) no positive claim survives
        the authors' own evaluation; (ii) the named architecture
        is empirically falsified; (iii) the cohort is structurally
        below the minimum scale needed to support any conclusion
        about the central question.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Summary of the Submission}

This manuscript studies whether chest CT can serve as a
training-only privileged modality for improving an X-ray-only
deployed classifier under a same-patient paired cohort setting.
The paper formulates CT-to-X-ray transfer as a cross-modal
knowledge distillation problem and evaluates multiple
distillation variants under a repeated patient-level Monte Carlo
protocol.

The study compares student-only learning, same-modality KD,
late fusion, plain cross-modal logit KD, attention transfer,
feature hints, and several generic KD baselines including
CRD, DKD, DIST, and modality-hallucination KD.
The proposed JDCNet architecture further introduces three
optional modules (DPE, MHRA, DFPN).

The paper’s central claim is intentionally conservative:
plain cross-modal logit KD and attention transfer show
directional improvement over the X-ray-only supervised baseline,
but the gains are not statistically significant at the current cohort scale.
The manuscript further reports that the proposed module stack
does not improve upon the stripped-down logit-transfer baseline.

The paper is unusually transparent about negative results,
evaluation fragility, and evidential limitations.

\section*{Overall Recommendation}

\textbf{Major Revision}

\medskip

This manuscript addresses an important and clinically relevant
question: whether a richer modality (CT) can improve a deployment-realistic
X-ray-only classifier through training-time-only supervision.
The paper also places unusual emphasis on evidence robustness,
same-case resampling, and negative-result transparency.

Compared with a TCSVT submission, the manuscript is substantially
better aligned with the scope of IEEE TMI because the core contribution
is fundamentally methodological and evaluation-centric within
medical imaging.

However, in its current form, the paper still has several major weaknesses
that prevent immediate acceptance:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item the paired cohort is extremely small and statistically underpowered;
    \item the primary positive claim is not statistically validated;
    \item the proposed architectural modules are empirically unsupported;
    \item the manuscript mixes protocol contribution,
          architectural contribution, and negative-result reporting
          without sufficiently prioritizing them;
    \item the clinical and imaging interpretation remains underdeveloped.
\end{itemize}

Despite these issues, the manuscript has several strengths:
excellent reproducibility infrastructure, unusually careful
uncertainty reporting, transparent negative-result analysis,
and a clinically meaningful deployment framing.
With substantial restructuring and clearer positioning,
this work could become a valuable methodological paper
for the medical-imaging community.

\subsection{The manuscript lacks a statistically validated primary finding}

The most important issue is that the paper’s main scientific claim
remains statistically unsupported.

The manuscript repeatedly reports that:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item plain cross-modal logit KD achieves
          balanced accuracy $0.900 \pm 0.211$;
    \item the X-ray-only baseline achieves
          $0.800 \pm 0.258$;
    \item however, the difference is not statistically significant
          ($p = 0.625$, Wilcoxon signed-rank test, $n = 10$).
\end{itemize}

This creates a fundamental problem for a TMI paper:
the manuscript carefully demonstrates that the central
cross-modal advantage has not yet been validated.

The paper therefore currently reads more as:

\begin{quote}
``an evidence-boundary analysis showing that the present
cohort is insufficient to establish CT-to-X-ray transfer''
\end{quote}

than as a validated algorithmic contribution.

The authors should therefore explicitly decide which of the
following directions they want the paper to emphasize:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item a negative-result / evidence-boundary paper;
    \item a reproducibility and evaluation methodology paper;
    \item a clinically motivated transfer-learning study;
    \item or an architecture paper.
\end{enumerate}

At present, the manuscript attempts to do all four simultaneously,
which weakens the scientific narrative.

%---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The paired cohort is too small for reliable clinical conclusions}

The paired cohort contains:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item 19 patients;
    \item 26 X-ray images;
    \item only 4 negative patients;
    \item one negative validation patient per resample.
\end{itemize}

This is an extremely severe limitation.

Specificity-sensitive metrics such as:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item specificity,
    \item MCC,
    \item balanced accuracy,
\end{itemize}

become highly unstable under this regime.

Indeed, the reported standard deviations
($\pm 0.422$, $\pm 0.527$) largely reflect
single-example Bernoulli variance rather than
true uncertainty about model ranking.

The authors acknowledge this limitation,
which is appreciated,
but the manuscript still presents extensive ranking analysis
that risks over-interpretation.

The repeated-resampling protocol improves methodological rigor,
but it does not solve the underlying sample-support problem.

For a TMI paper,
the current evidence scale is below the standard expected
for establishing clinically meaningful conclusions.

The BIMCV stress-test appendix is therefore critically important,
yet it remains secondary rather than central in the current paper structure.

%---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The architectural contribution is empirically unsupported}

The proposed JDCNet architecture introduces:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item DPE;
    \item MHRA;
    \item DFPN.
\end{itemize}

However, the experiments consistently show that:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Full JDCNet underperforms plain logit KD;
    \item module addition monotonically degrades performance;
    \item the simpler stripped-down baseline is the strongest method.
\end{itemize}

The manuscript itself repeatedly acknowledges this result.

Therefore, the current paper cannot simultaneously claim:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item that JDCNet is the proposed architecture;
    \item and that the architecture is unsupported.
\end{enumerate}

This creates narrative inconsistency.

The authors should strongly consider reframing the paper around:

\begin{quote}
``cross-modal evidence-bounded evaluation''
\end{quote}

rather than around the JDCNet module stack.

The current title over-emphasizes the architecture.

%---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The clinical interpretation remains weak}

Although the paper is framed as a medical-imaging study,
the clinical implications are underdeveloped.

Specifically:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item there is limited radiological interpretation;
    \item no analysis of which CT findings transfer successfully;
    \item no investigation of disease pattern correspondence;
    \item no error analysis from a clinical perspective;
    \item no subgroup analysis;
    \item no discussion of lesion visibility mismatch between CT and X-ray.
\end{itemize}

The paper currently treats CT as a generic stronger modality,
but does not analyze:

\begin{quote}
\emph{what information is actually transferred.}
\end{quote}

For TMI,
stronger imaging interpretation is expected.

The paper would benefit substantially from:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Grad-CAM / saliency analysis;
    \item teacher-student attention comparison;
    \item visualization of successful and failed transfer cases;
    \item analysis of lesion patterns visible on CT but absent on X-ray;
    \item clinically interpretable failure modes.
\end{itemize}

%---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The manuscript overuses evidential language}

Terms such as:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item ``evidence-boundary'';
    \item ``falsification-oriented'';
    \item ``evidential floor'';
    \item ``claim boundary'';
    \item ``directional but not significant'';
\end{itemize}

appear extremely frequently.

While the transparency is commendable,
the manuscript sometimes reads more like
a philosophy-of-science discussion
than a medical-imaging paper.

The paper should reduce repetitive meta-discussion
and focus more directly on:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item imaging findings;
    \item transfer mechanisms;
    \item methodological implications;
    \item clinical deployment relevance.
\end{itemize}

%---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The BIMCV experiments should be moved into the main paper}

The BIMCV experiments are currently treated as a secondary appendix stress test.

However, these experiments are actually the most scientifically important part
of the paper because they address the core limitation:
tiny-cohort instability.

The manuscript should restructure the evidence hierarchy:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item BIMCV repeated-resampling should become the primary evidence;
    \item the tiny paired cohort should become the pilot feasibility study;
    \item the current appendix material should be partially moved into the main text.
\end{enumerate}

At present, the paper gives disproportionate emphasis
to the tiny 19-patient cohort.

%---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The manuscript lacks comparison with modern medical foundation models}

The baseline section is incomplete relative to current TMI standards.

The paper compares against:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item CRD,
    \item DKD,
    \item DIST,
    \item modality hallucination KD.
\end{itemize}

However, there is insufficient engagement with:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item MedCLIP;
    \item BiomedCLIP;
    \item RadImageNet-pretrained models;
    \item multimodal radiology foundation models;
    \item recent medical contrastive learning frameworks.
\end{itemize}

Even if such models cannot be fully trained under the current cohort size,
their role in the literature should be discussed more deeply.

%---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The deployment framing is clinically plausible but technically incomplete}

The X-ray-only deployment framing is reasonable and clinically meaningful.

However, the deployment analysis remains shallow.

Specifically:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item no robustness analysis;
    \item no domain-shift evaluation;
    \item no external clinical validation;
    \item no uncertainty calibration study;
    \item no deployment-failure analysis.
\end{itemize}

The cross-source collapse reported in the appendix
is actually extremely important,
yet it is not sufficiently foregrounded.

The manuscript should discuss much more explicitly that:

\begin{quote}
shortcut learning and source-specific acquisition bias
may dominate the learned transfer signal.
\end{quote}

\section{Questions for the Authors}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]

\item
What is the primary intended contribution of the paper:
architecture, evaluation methodology,
negative-result analysis,
or deployment-oriented medical transfer learning?

\item
Why is the tiny paired cohort still treated as the primary evidence source
when the BIMCV stress-test experiments are substantially more informative?

\item
What imaging features appear transferable from CT to X-ray?
Can the authors visualize successful transfer cases?

\item
Why does attention transfer match plain logit KD almost exactly?
Does this imply that the transfer signal is mostly logit-level?

\item
Do the authors believe the current evidence supports any clinically meaningful
cross-modal advantage?

\item
Could the observed transfer behavior simply reflect acquisition bias
rather than disease-specific transfer?

\item
Why are the proposed modules retained in the paper title and abstract
if the experiments consistently show that they do not help?

\end{enumerate}
