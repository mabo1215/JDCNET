\section*{Review for IEEE Transactions on Circuits and Systems for Video Technology (TCSVT)}

\subsection*{Summary}

This paper studies a clinically motivated and deployment-relevant problem:
whether chest CT can serve as a training-only privileged modality to improve an X-ray-only classifier under a same-patient paired setting.
The authors formulate the problem as cross-modal knowledge distillation (KD),
where CT acts as the teacher modality and chest X-ray acts as the student modality.

The paper introduces a framework called JDCNet and evaluates multiple transfer configurations under a repeated patient-level Monte Carlo resampling protocol.
A major contribution claimed by the paper is not architectural superiority itself,
but rather an ``evidence-bounded'' evaluation methodology intended to prevent over-interpretation of unstable gains on extremely small paired cohorts.

The manuscript is carefully written and unusually transparent about its own evidential limitations.
The authors explicitly distinguish:
(1) directional observations,
(2) statistically supported findings,
and
(3) unsupported architectural claims.

The study is therefore best interpreted as a falsification-oriented pilot analysis rather than a definitive multimodal medical-imaging benchmark.

\vspace{1em}

\subsection*{Strengths}

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Scientifically honest framing and evidence-boundary awareness.}

One of the strongest aspects of this work is that the authors explicitly avoid overstating conclusions.
The manuscript repeatedly emphasizes that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the paired cohort is extremely small,
    \item the validation splits are prevalence-heavy,
    \item many observed gains are directional rather than statistically significant,
    \item and the proposed architectural stack is not empirically validated.
\end{itemize}

This level of evidential transparency is rare in medical imaging papers and significantly improves the credibility of the work.

\item \textbf{Well-designed same-case resampling protocol.}

The repeated patient-level Monte Carlo resampling framework is the most technically valuable contribution of the paper.
The protocol:
\begin{itemize}
    \item evaluates all methods on identical held-out patients,
    \item explicitly reports imbalance-sensitive metrics,
    \item and avoids relying solely on optimistic fixed-split rankings.
\end{itemize}

This addresses a major weakness frequently seen in tiny-cohort medical imaging studies.

\item \textbf{Strong reproducibility effort.}

The manuscript provides:
\begin{itemize}
    \item detailed appendix-level implementation descriptions,
    \item parameter-count analysis,
    \item calibration diagnostics,
    \item threshold-sensitivity studies,
    \item robustness reporting,
    \item power analysis,
    \item and Code Ocean reproducibility support.
\end{itemize}

The reproducibility standard is substantially above average for medical-imaging KD papers.

\item \textbf{Negative-result reporting is valuable.}

The paper shows that several generic KD methods (CRD, DKD, DIST, modality hallucination KD) collapse toward trivial all-positive prediction regimes under the paired setting.
This negative result is important because many prior medical KD studies would likely omit such findings.

\item \textbf{The paper fits TCSVT better than purely clinical journals.}

The contribution is fundamentally about:
\begin{itemize}
    \item cross-modal representation transfer,
    \item deployment-efficient visual inference,
    \item distillation mechanisms,
    \item and evidence-aware evaluation methodology.
\end{itemize}

These topics align with TCSVT's multimedia and visual computing scope.

\end{enumerate}

\vspace{1em}

\subsection*{Weaknesses}

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{The primary paired cohort is still extremely underpowered.}

Despite the careful framing, the core limitation remains severe:
the main paired validation protocol contains only one negative validation patient per resample.

This makes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item specificity,
    \item MCC,
    \item calibration estimates,
    \item threshold analysis,
    \item and statistical testing
\end{itemize}
highly unstable.

Even though the authors openly acknowledge this limitation,
it still substantially weakens the strength of the empirical claims.

\item \textbf{The main claimed positive signal is not statistically significant.}

The central result of the paper is that plain cross-modal KD and attention transfer achieve mean balanced accuracy of 0.900 versus 0.800 for the supervised baseline.

However:
\[
p = 0.625
\]
under the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

Therefore, the paper ultimately cannot statistically demonstrate that CT-to-X-ray transfer improves over supervised X-ray-only learning.

This substantially limits the impact of the contribution.

\item \textbf{The proposed JDCNet architecture itself is not validated.}

Ironically, the paper's own results show that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Full JDCNet underperforms plain KD,
    \item MHRA appears harmful,
    \item additional complexity increases parameters and latency,
    \item and the stripped-down baseline is consistently stronger.
\end{itemize}

Thus the evidence does not currently support the proposed architectural stack as a meaningful advance over simple logit transfer.

\item \textbf{Clinical relevance remains weak.}

The paper repeatedly states that no clinical-readiness claim is made.
While this honesty is appreciated, it also means the work remains primarily methodological and exploratory.

The cohort size and dataset construction are insufficient for any meaningful medical conclusion.

\item \textbf{The BIMCV stress-test still fails to validate transfer effectiveness.}

The larger 512-patient BIMCV paired cohort improves experimental scale,
but even there:
\begin{itemize}
    \item cross-modal KD remains unstable,
    \item the gain over supervised baselines remains statistically insignificant,
    \item and prototype-based KD still fails to demonstrate reliable improvement.
\end{itemize}

Therefore the paper does not yet establish that CT-to-X-ray transfer is genuinely beneficial.

\item \textbf{The contribution is more about evaluation methodology than about algorithmic novelty.}

From an algorithmic perspective:
\begin{itemize}
    \item DPE,
    \item MHRA,
    \item DFPN
\end{itemize}
are relatively lightweight combinations of existing operations.

The stronger contribution lies in evidential framing and falsification methodology rather than in architectural innovation itself.

\end{enumerate}

\vspace{1em}

\subsection*{Technical Comments}

\begin{enumerate}

\item The manuscript should more explicitly distinguish:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ``cross-modal transfer exists''
    from
    \item ``plain KD is merely more robust than other KD methods under severe imbalance''.
\end{itemize}

At present, the latter interpretation is arguably better supported by the evidence.

\item The repeated emphasis on ``directional but not significant'' findings is scientifically appropriate,
but the paper may benefit from a more concise presentation in several sections.
Some discussions become repetitive.

\item The calibration analysis is useful,
but because:
\[
n_{\text{neg}} = 1
\]
per resample,
ECE and reliability diagrams should be interpreted extremely cautiously.
The manuscript should state this even more strongly.

\item The discussion around threshold-collapse behavior is one of the strongest parts of the paper and should potentially be moved earlier into the main text.

\item The ImageNet-pretrained ResNet18 baseline achieving perfect performance on the fixed split strongly highlights how fragile the original split is.
This point deserves stronger discussion.

\item The manuscript should clarify whether CT slice selection could itself leak label bias.
Using the ``most central axial slice'' may unintentionally encode acquisition-specific shortcuts.

\item The manuscript should discuss whether the transfer signal may largely originate from:
\begin{itemize}
    \item prevalence bias,
    \item acquisition-style correlation,
    \item or scanner/domain artifacts,
\end{itemize}
rather than disease semantics.

\item The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion comparing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item privileged-information learning,
    \item modality hallucination,
    \item and cross-modal KD
\end{itemize}
from a theoretical perspective.

Currently the framing remains mostly empirical.

\end{enumerate}

\vspace{1em}

\subsection*{Minor Comments}

\begin{enumerate}

\item Several sections are overly long and could be condensed for readability.

\item The manuscript occasionally repeats the same cautionary statements multiple times.

\item Some appendix tables are extremely detailed and may exceed what is necessary for the main narrative.

\item The notation around ``cross-modal KD'', ``cross-modality KD'', and ``plain KD'' should be standardized.

\item The manuscript would benefit from a dedicated ``Threats to Validity'' subsection.

\item Figure captions are informative but sometimes excessively verbose.

\end{enumerate}

\vspace{1em}

\subsection*{Recommendation}

\textbf{Recommendation: Weak Accept / Borderline Accept}

\vspace{0.5em}

This paper is unusual because its strongest contribution is not a positive algorithmic result,
but rather a careful demonstration of how easily tiny paired-cohort medical-imaging studies can produce unstable or misleading transfer claims.

The empirical evidence does \emph{not} currently validate:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the superiority of JDCNet,
    \item nor statistically significant CT-to-X-ray transfer gains.
\end{itemize}

However, the manuscript still provides value through:
\begin{itemize}
    \item rigorous same-case resampling methodology,
    \item transparent falsification-oriented analysis,
    \item unusually strong reproducibility,
    \item and explicit reporting of negative results.
\end{itemize}

The paper therefore contributes more as an evidence-robustness and evaluation methodology study than as a definitive multimodal medical-imaging method paper.

For TCSVT specifically, the paper may be acceptable if the editors and reviewers value:
\begin{itemize}
    \item reproducibility,
    \item honest negative-result reporting,
    \item and evidence-aware multimedia evaluation methodology.
\end{itemize}

The manuscript should \emph{not} be interpreted as establishing clinically validated cross-modal transfer effectiveness.

\vspace{1em}
