\section{Editorial Recommendation}

\begin{majorbox}
\textbf{Overall recommendation:} \textit{Major Revision} (with strong reservation about scope-of-venue fit).\\[2pt]
\textbf{Confidence:} 4 / 5.\\[2pt]
\textbf{Headline reason:} The submission is methodologically honest and the evidence-boundary framing is unusual and useful, but as currently positioned the manuscript (i) does not deliver a positive contribution that meets the methodological-novelty bar typical of IEEE TCSVT, (ii) draws conclusions from a paired cohort whose validation support (4 fixed-split images; 8 Monte Carlo resamples with 1 negative validation patient each) is below the threshold required for a stand-alone empirical contribution, and (iii) under-positions itself relative to TCSVT's image/video processing scope. The paper is salvageable, but a credible revision must enlarge the evidence base (BIMCV-COVID19+ should be folded into the headline tables, not deferred), strengthen the baseline grid (modern Transformer/CLIP-style backbones; published cross-modal distillation methods), and either deliver a positive, generalizable methodological contribution \emph{or} re-target a venue whose remit explicitly accepts negative-result evidence-protocol papers.
\end{majorbox}

\vspace{4pt}

\noindent\textbf{Recommended decision tree:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item If the authors elect to keep JDCNet as the headline contribution \(\Rightarrow\) \emph{reject and resubmit}: a tiny-cohort pilot whose own modules do not help cannot anchor a TCSVT submission.
  \item If the authors elect to keep the evidence-boundary framing as the headline contribution \(\Rightarrow\) \emph{major revision}: the protocol must be demonstrated on (a) BIMCV-COVID19+ with at least 50 validation patients and a positive/negative ratio of at most 4:1, and (b) at least one additional thoracic dataset (e.g.\ MIMIC-CXR / CheXpert subset for the X-ray side). \end{itemize}

\section{Summary of the Submission}

The manuscript studies whether same-patient chest CT can serve as \emph{training-only} privileged supervision for an X-ray-only deployment classifier on a binary COVID-19 vs.\ non-COVID task. The authors:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Define a tiny patient-level paired cohort (26 X-ray images / 19 patients; 4-image validation split with 3 positives and 1 negative).
  \item Introduce a CNN-based teacher--student scaffold (\textit{JDCNet}) consisting of three optional modules---Dynamic Positional Encoding (DPE), Multi-Head Relation Aggregation (MHRA), and Dense Feature Pyramid Network (DFPN)---over a generalized-distillation objective.
  \item Run a fixed-split feasibility screen (4 seeds) and a primary same-case Monte Carlo resampling study (8 splits).
  \item Add mechanism controls (attention transfer~\cite{zagoruyko2017attention}, feature hints~\cite{romero2014fitnets}), late fusion, same-modality KD, plain logit KD, and a category-level cross-source non-COVID control.
  \item Conclude that no robust CT-to-X-ray transfer signal survives same-case resampling, that DPE/MHRA/DFPN do not improve over plain logit KD, and that the paper's contribution should be read as an \emph{evidence-bounded protocol} rather than as a validated architecture.
\end{enumerate}

The framing is intellectually honest and uncommon. The paper repeatedly self-limits its claims (Tables I, IV, and the hypothesis-status table). The reviewer respects this stance; the remainder of this report addresses whether the package is currently strong enough for IEEE TCSVT.

\section{Strengths}

\begin{minorbox}
\textbf{S1. Honest, falsification-oriented framing.} The hypothesis-status table (\texttt{tab:hypothesis\_status}) explicitly marks H2--H5 as ``not supported.'' This is rare and methodologically valuable.\\[3pt]
\textbf{S2. Same-case repeated resampling.} Evaluating every method on the identical held-out patients within each Monte Carlo split is the right design for a tiny-cohort comparison and is well-motivated against fixed-split accident.\\[3pt]
\textbf{S3. Clear scope contract.} \texttt{tab:problem\_scope} and \texttt{tab:evaluation\_regimes} are well-designed: they define the evidential role of each regime up front and discipline the rest of the paper.\\[3pt]
\textbf{S4. Distillation is correctly framed as privileged information.} Citing \cite{vapnik2009lupi,lopezpaz2016privileged,hoffman2016hallucination} grounds the deployment-only X-ray constraint correctly; many cross-modal medical papers conflate this with multimodal fusion.\\[3pt]
\textbf{S5. Reproducibility scaffolding.} Manifest generation, patient-level audit (\texttt{tab:split\_audit}), per-seed table (\texttt{tab:paired\_seed\_results}), and the implementation-details table (\texttt{tab:implementation\_details}) make the pipeline auditable.\\[3pt]
\textbf{S6. Negative-result transparency.} Reporting the all-positive collapse with $\pm 0.000$ standard deviation and the threshold-band collapse is candid and informative.
\end{minorbox}

\section{Major Concerns}

\subsection{M1. Venue fit (IEEE TCSVT) is asserted, not demonstrated}

\begin{majorbox}
TCSVT's editorial scope centres on \emph{circuits, systems, and algorithms for video and image technology}, with strong emphasis on signal-processing innovation, real-time/efficient inference architectures, perceptual coding, and multimedia. The Motivation paragraph claims fit by gesturing at ``deep learning for biomedical image classification'' (lines 123--134), but does not deliver any of the deliverables a TCSVT reviewer expects:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item No FLOPs / latency / memory analysis on an embedded or edge-class device.
  \item No video-temporal component (single 2-D slices from CT volumes; no axial/temporal fusion).
  \item No coding/compression/transmission angle.
  \item No quantitative claim about deployment efficiency beyond ``X-ray only at inference.''
\end{itemize}
The 128$\times$128 input resolution and 5-epoch training schedule further weaken any efficiency argument because the system is too small to be a credible deployment artefact.
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} Either (a) re-target a venue whose remit covers tiny-cohort medical-imaging evidence protocols (e.g.\ \emph{Medical Image Analysis}, \emph{IEEE TMI}, \emph{IEEE JBHI}), or (b) substantially rebuild the deployment-efficiency argument: include FLOPs, parameter counts, latency on at least one CPU and one mobile/edge GPU, and demonstrate the protocol on a video- or volumetric-temporal CT signal so that the ``video technology'' framing is not nominal. The current Motivation paragraph (lines 123--126) is the single weakest paragraph of the paper.

\subsection{M2. Sample size is below the evidence threshold for any empirical claim}

\begin{majorbox}
The manuscript repeatedly self-flags the sample-size problem, which is appreciated, but the underlying numbers are below the threshold at which any reviewer can be persuaded that the protocol \emph{itself} (the ostensible contribution) is reliable. Specifically:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Paired fixed split: 4 validation images, 1 negative validation patient.
  \item Monte Carlo resamples: 8 resamples, each with 1 negative patient by construction (line 295). With $n_{\text{neg}}=1$, specificity per resample is binary (0 or 1), so the reported $\text{Specificity}=0.500\pm0.535$ is mechanically a count of how many resamples correctly classify a single image.
  \item Macro-F1 and balanced accuracy on a 5-image to 10-image validation split with one negative patient inherit the same fragility.
  \item Hyperparameter ablation across $T \in \{2,4,6\}$ and $\alpha \in \{0.3,0.6,0.9\}$ all collapse to the same number (line 374), confirming that the cohort is below the discriminative resolution.
\end{itemize}
At this scale, the reported rankings cannot distinguish between ``signal that does not exist,'' ``signal that exists but is masked by noise,'' and ``protocol that is too coarse to detect signal.''  The author's own diagnosis (line 374) acknowledges this. The paper therefore needs evidence at a scale where the protocol can produce non-degenerate metrics before it can claim that the protocol is the contribution.
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Promote the \textbf{BIMCV-COVID19+} prepared subset (638 same-patient COVID positives; 3{,}080 radiographs) into the main result tables. The current deferral (lines 257--258, 415, 294) is the central weakness of the empirical section.
  \item Construct a \emph{paired non-COVID} arm by treating ``other thoracic finding'' or ``no acute finding with paired CT'' as the negative class. The current binary COVID/non-COVID definition is what blocks BIMCV; relaxing the label space (or using a pneumonia-vs-COVID split with same-patient CT) would enable a 50-patient evaluation.
  \item Increase resamples to $\geq 30$ and require per-resample $n_{\text{neg}} \geq 5$. With $n_{\text{neg}}=1$, the standard-deviation columns in Table~IV are not statistically interpretable.
  \item Use BCa bootstrap confidence intervals (\emph{not} normal-approximation) on the resample distribution; report the per-resample paired-difference test (sign test or Wilcoxon signed-rank) for any pairwise claim such as ``late fusion $>$ student-only.''
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{M3. The proposed method does not work, and the paper does not pivot cleanly}

\begin{majorbox}
JDCNet is introduced architecturally (Sec.~III.B, Fig.~1) and named (the authors explicitly acknowledge they coined DPE, MHRA, and DFPN, line 224). Yet:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Tables III and IV both show that the full JDCNet stack \emph{underperforms} the plain logit-KD baseline.
  \item The progressive-complexity table (\texttt{tab:progressive\_complexity}) shows monotonic degradation as DPE/MHRA/DFPN are added.
  \item The hypothesis-status table marks H4 (``DPE/MHRA/DFPN improve over plain control'') as ``not supported.''
\end{itemize}
A TCSVT reader's reasonable inference is then: \emph{why is JDCNet in the paper at all?} The current answer (``executable pilot scaffold'' / ``hypothesis generators'') is not a methodological contribution suitable for TCSVT. The branding of three new module names that have no demonstrated benefit is also editorially unusual; reviewers will read it as introducing terminology faster than evidence.
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} Choose one of the following pivots, and execute it consistently across abstract, contributions list, Section~III, Section~IV, and Conclusion:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Pivot to a positive method.} Replace the lightweight CNN scaffold with a TCSVT-appropriate efficient architecture (e.g., MobileNetV3 / EfficientFormer / TinyViT student; ResNet-50 or ConvNeXt-Tiny teacher) and report a \emph{positive} cross-modal transfer result on a sufficiently large paired cohort. In this case, drop DPE/MHRA/DFPN unless they provide non-trivial gains in the new regime.
  \item \textbf{Pivot to a pure protocol paper.} Remove DPE/MHRA/DFPN from the contributions list, the architecture figure, and the abstract. Rewrite Section~III as ``Evaluation Protocol'' rather than ``Methodology.'' In this version, JDCNet does not exist as a named architecture; the artefact is the protocol plus reference baselines.
\end{enumerate}
The current half-and-half presentation (a named architecture that the authors then disclaim) is the single most damaging editorial choice in the manuscript.

\subsection{M4. Baseline coverage is too narrow for a 2026 cross-modal-distillation paper}

\begin{majorbox}
The compared methods are: student-only X-ray, late fusion, same-modality KD, plain logit KD~\cite{hinton2015distilling}, attention transfer~\cite{zagoruyko2017attention}, feature hints~\cite{romero2014fitnets}, and JDCNet. The most recent of these is from 2017. Cross-modal medical distillation has moved on substantially:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item No comparison to \textbf{Modality Hallucination}~\cite{hoffman2016hallucination}, which is cited (line 183) but not implemented as a baseline despite being the closest published method to the proposed setting.
  \item No comparison to \textbf{Cross-Modal Distillation for Supervision Transfer}~\cite{gupta2016crossmodal}, again cited but not run.
  \item No comparison to \textbf{Contrastive Representation Distillation}~\cite{tian2020contrastive}, cited but not run.
  \item No CLIP / BiomedCLIP / MedCLIP-style pretraining baselines, which currently define the strong-supervised reference for chest-X-ray classification.
  \item No comparison to relation-based KD (RKD), correlation-congruence KD (CCKD), or PKT.
  \item No comparison to a privileged-information SVM+ or a ranking-based privileged loss from the LUPI literature.
\end{itemize}
The Limitations section concedes this (lines 429--430), but a TCSVT reviewer will not accept ``stronger baselines deferred to next-cohort'' when the paper's central claim is precisely that no transfer signal survives.
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} Add at minimum: (i) modality hallucination~\cite{hoffman2016hallucination}; (ii) cross-modal distillation~\cite{gupta2016crossmodal}; (iii) CRD~\cite{tian2020contrastive}; (iv) one CLIP-family pretrained baseline (e.g.\ BiomedCLIP) used as a frozen feature extractor for the X-ray student; (v) one strong same-modality teacher (DenseNet-121 in CheXNet style, or ConvNeXt-Tiny). The current ``attention transfer + feature hint'' pair is too thin to support the H1--H5 claim board.

\subsection{M5. Architecture choices are below current TCSVT-appropriate practice}

\begin{majorbox}
The implementation details (\texttt{tab:implementation\_details}) report:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Teacher: 4-stage CNN (32/64/128/256 channels). Total \(\sim\)1.29M parameters.
  \item Student: 3-stage CNN (32/64/128 channels). \(\sim\)0.48M parameters.
  \item Input: $128 \times 128$. Epochs: 5. Optimizer: AdamW, lr $3\times10^{-4}$.
\end{itemize}
A 5-epoch training budget on a CNN that has never seen ImageNet pretraining cannot be expected to converge on a binary COVID task even on a large cohort, let alone on 22 training images. The reviewer therefore cannot rule out that the negative cross-modal result is an under-training artefact rather than a transfer failure. This is a serious confound.
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} (a) initialize teacher and student from ImageNet (or RadImageNet, which is more apt) pretrained weights; (b) increase the training budget to convergence (track validation loss; stop on plateau); (c) include a learning-rate warmup and cosine schedule; (d) report training and validation loss curves so the reader can verify convergence; (e) raise input resolution to at least $224\times 224$, which is the current standard for medical-image classification.

\subsection{M6. Statistical reporting is not numerically interpretable in the current regime}

\begin{majorbox}
The paper writes ``$\pm$ standard deviation across resamples'' but with $n_{\text{neg}}=1$ per split, the standard deviation column for specificity is mechanically the binomial standard deviation of a single Bernoulli draw repeated 8 times. This is not a stable estimator of variability across patients. The same critique applies to balanced accuracy and MCC. Additionally:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item No paired statistical test between methods is reported in the main text. The appendix explicitly declines to use McNemar (line 290), but a sign test or Wilcoxon over the 8 paired resample differences is feasible and would be more convincing than mean-only comparisons.
  \item PR-AUC is reported but the prevalence ($\sim$80\% positive) makes PR-AUC easily inflated. Report PR-AUC \emph{relative to the prevalence baseline} ($\Delta$PR-AUC) or use prevalence-corrected metrics.
  \item The four-seed standard deviations on the fixed split (Table~III) are computed over only 4 random seeds on 4 validation images. The per-seed identical-result phenomenon (line 60 of the appendix, seeds 42 and 43) shows that two of those four ``replicates'' are not independent.
\end{itemize}
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} Replace mean$\pm$SD reporting with: (i) median + IQR over resamples; (ii) BCa bootstrap 95\% CI; (iii) paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test for each pairwise method comparison; (iv) explicit reporting of per-resample $n_{\text{pos}}$ and $n_{\text{neg}}$ in a supplementary table. Drop PR-AUC unless reported as $\Delta$ vs.\ prevalence baseline.

\subsection{M7. The category-level cross-source non-COVID control is a confound, not a control}

\begin{majorbox}
The supplementary Table~A3 / \texttt{tab:noncovid\_control} reports applying paired-cohort-trained models to a Kaggle ``chest-xray-pneumonia'' NORMAL set. The result (sensitivity 1.00, specificity 0.00--0.32) is described as ``confirming severe distribution shift'' (line 303). The reviewer agrees with the framing but disagrees that this control adds evidential value: it confirms only that classifiers trained on a 22-image cohort do not transfer to an unrelated source, which is unsurprising and confounded by acquisition, demographics, and label-noise differences. Including this table in the appendix is acceptable, but the wording in the main-paper Limitations section (lines 427--428) currently overweights what the control demonstrates.
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} Either (a) remove the non-COVID cross-source control from the main-paper Limitations and restrict it to a clearly-labelled appendix curiosity; or (b) replace it with a same-source negative class (e.g., Cohen-collection non-COVID radiographs from patients without paired CT) so the comparison is acquisition-matched.

\subsection{M8. Reproducibility statement is incomplete}

\begin{majorbox}
The paper claims (lines 413--415) that all manifests, scripts, and figures are produced by an executable pipeline. However, the manuscript does not provide:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A code release URL (anonymized for review).
  \item A version-pinned environment file (\texttt{environment.yml}, \texttt{requirements.txt}) or Docker image.
  \item Hardware/runtime details (GPU model, training time).
  \item A data-license statement for the COVID-19 Image Data Collection and BIMCV redistribution restrictions.
  \item Random-seed control statement (deterministic algorithms? CuDNN benchmark off?).
\end{itemize}
For a paper whose central contribution is methodological reproducibility, this is a significant gap.
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} Add a Reproducibility subsection to the appendix containing all eight items above. Provide an anonymized GitHub or Zenodo link compatible with double-blind review (e.g., \texttt{anonymous.4open.science}).

\subsection{M9. The KL-divergence direction and the gradient claim are inconsistent with the equation as written}

\begin{majorbox}
Equation (1) on lines 233--240 is written as
\[
L = (1-\alpha)\,\mathrm{CE}(f_S(x^{(\mathrm{xr})}), y) + \alpha T^2\, \mathrm{KL}\!\left(\sigma(f_T(x^{(\mathrm{ct})})/T) \,\|\, \sigma(f_S(x^{(\mathrm{xr})})/T)\right).
\]
Line 241 states ``the KL term is written in the teacher-to-student direction $\mathrm{KL}(p_T \| p_S)$.'' This is the \emph{forward} (zero-avoiding) KL, which forces the student to cover all teacher modes. Hinton et al.~\cite{hinton2015distilling} use cross-entropy $H(p_T, p_S)$, which is equivalent up to a teacher-only constant. Two issues:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item The PyTorch \texttt{torch.nn.KLDivLoss} default convention is $\mathrm{KL}(p_S \| p_T)$ (i.e., reverse KL when $p_T$ is the second argument). It is unclear whether the implemented loss matches the equation as written. Many published distillation implementations get this wrong silently.
  \item The claim that ``gradients flow only to the student parameters'' is correct only if $f_T$ is in \texttt{eval()} mode and \texttt{detach()} is applied to the teacher logits. The paper does not state that this is the case.
\end{itemize}
\end{majorbox}

\textbf{Required action:} (a) Add an unambiguous code listing of the loss in the appendix; (b) state explicitly whether the teacher is frozen and detached during the student update; (c) verify that the implemented direction matches the equation.

\subsection{M10. Single dataset; no external generalization}

The entirety of the empirical evidence comes from one curated source (Cohen et al.~\cite{cohen2020covid,cohen2020covidProspective}). The dataset's authors warn against treating it as a benchmark (acknowledged in line 250). For a paper whose contribution is a \emph{protocol}, the protocol must be demonstrated on at least two independent datasets to support any generalization claim.

\textbf{Required action:} Run the protocol on at least one additional thoracic dataset that has same-patient paired CT/X-ray, e.g.\ MIDRC, BIMCV-COVID19+ (already prepared), or a curated subset of MIMIC-CXR with linked CT records. The current single-source design cannot establish protocol generality.

\section{Moderate Concerns}

\subsection{O1. Figure 1 is described but not analyzed}

The architecture overview (Fig.~1, line 214) lists DPE / MHRA / DFPN as ``optional auditable complexity increments,'' but the figure itself implies a designed pipeline. Once the empirical section concludes that these modules do not help, the figure becomes misleading. \textbf{Action:} either redraw Fig.~1 as a \emph{baseline comparison diagram} (showing student-only, late fusion, same-modality KD, plain KD, JDCNet side-by-side) or remove the architecture diagram entirely if the protocol-paper pivot (M3) is taken.

\subsection{O2. Threshold sweep analysis is descriptive, not actionable}

The threshold analysis (Fig.~5; appendix lines 217--223) shows that all models behave bimodally around 0.5--0.6. This is presented as evidence of fragility, but no threshold-calibration intervention is attempted. \textbf{Action:} report results at the prevalence-matched optimal threshold (\texttt{argmax} balanced accuracy on the validation set) and at the Youden-J-optimal threshold; this would convert the diagnostic into an actionable mitigation.

\subsection{O3. ``Late fusion attains the highest mean accuracy'' is a misleading headline}

The phrase appears in the abstract, the resampling section, and the conclusion. Late fusion at 0.885 mean accuracy with 0.250 mean specificity (Table~IV, line 355) is essentially predicting all positives in a prevalence-heavy regime. The paper does flag this, but the headline metric in the abstract reinforces the wrong takeaway. \textbf{Action:} replace ``highest mean accuracy'' framing with ``highest mean accuracy but lowest mean specificity, consistent with prevalence-driven prediction collapse.''

\subsection{O4. The contributions list is misaligned with the actual deliverables}

Section~I.B lists three contributions: ``deployment-realistic problem formulation,'' ``executable pilot-study scaffold,'' and ``evidence-bounded empirical conclusion.'' Of these:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item C1 (formulation) is not novel; privileged information~\cite{vapnik2009lupi,lopezpaz2016privileged,hoffman2016hallucination} already covers it.
  \item C2 (pipeline) is reproducibility, not a research contribution.
  \item C3 is the only genuine contribution; it should be promoted to the headline.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Action:} rewrite the contributions list to lead with C3, drop C1, and reframe C2 as ``open-source reproducibility artefact'' (move to acknowledgements / data-availability).

\subsection{O5. Related-work coverage is dated on the cross-modal distillation side}

The Related Work section (lines 167--193) cites attention transfer (2017), FitNets (2014), CRD (2020), Hinton (2015), and a few medical-imaging-specific works. Missing recent literature includes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Decoupled Knowledge Distillation (CVPR 2022)
  \item Knowledge Distillation via Softmax Regression Representation Learning (ICLR 2021)
  \item DIST (Distillation from a Stronger Teacher, NeurIPS 2022)
  \item Cross-modal medical distillation papers from 2022--2024 (e.g., CT-to-X-ray for tuberculosis, MR-to-CT distillation)
  \item BiomedCLIP / MedCLIP / GLoRIA for chest-X-ray representation
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Action:} add a Related Work paragraph specifically on 2022--2025 distillation and on chest-X-ray foundation models.

\subsection{O6. The ``ranking instability'' claim could be quantified}

The paper repeatedly states that the fixed-split ranking is not preserved under resampling. This is a quantitative claim that should be supported by Kendall's $\tau$ or Spearman's $\rho$ between fixed-split rank order and resampled rank order. \textbf{Action:} add a 2-by-7 table reporting per-method rank under fixed split vs.\ resampling, plus the rank-correlation coefficient.

\subsection{O7. ``Anonymous Authors'' in the title block (line 85)}

For double-blind submission this is correct, but verify that TCSVT's current submission system uses double-blind. As of the most recent IEEE TCSVT author guidelines, TCSVT historically used single-blind; check the current Information for Authors. The commented-out author block (lines 87--93) reveals the institution names if the LaTeX comment is preserved in the submitted PDF---verify this is stripped.

\subsection{O8. Inconsistent terminology}

\subsection{Required new appendix subsection: code listing of the distillation loss}

\begin{lstlisting}
\subsection{Distillation loss reference implementation}
\begin{verbatim}
# teacher_logits: detached, computed under torch.no_grad()
# student_logits: requires gradient
import torch.nn.functional as F

def kd_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels, T, alpha):
    hard = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)
    p_T  = F.softmax(teacher_logits.detach() / T, dim=-1)
    log_p_S = F.log_softmax(student_logits / T, dim=-1)
    # KL(p_T || p_S) -- teacher is the reference, gradient on student
    soft = F.kl_div(log_p_S, p_T, reduction="batchmean") * (T * T)
    return (1 - alpha) * hard + alpha * soft
\end{verbatim}
The teacher is run under \texttt{torch.no\_grad()} and its logits are
detached prior to softmax, ensuring gradients flow only to the student
parameters. The KL direction is $\mathrm{KL}(p_T \| p_S)$ as written in
Eq.~(1), implemented via \texttt{F.kl\_div} with the student log-softmax
as the first argument and the teacher softmax as the target.
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Reviewer's Closing Note to the Authors}

\begin{notebox}
The reviewer wants to be explicit about the unusual nature of this submission. The authors are doing something methodologically valuable: they are running a falsification-oriented protocol on their own architecture and reporting that the architecture does not work. This is rare and the field benefits when it happens. The reviewer's recommendation of major revision is therefore not motivated by skepticism about the framing, but by three concerns:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A protocol paper must demonstrate the protocol on enough data that it produces non-degenerate measurements. The current paired cohort is below that threshold, and the BIMCV deferral leaves the headline empirical claim resting on 4 fixed-split images and 8 resamples with one negative patient each.
  \item A negative-result paper is hardest to publish at venues whose editorial culture rewards positive contributions. IEEE TCSVT historically has been such a venue. The authors should either (a) execute the protocol pivot fully and re-target a venue whose remit explicitly accepts negative-result protocol papers (e.g., \emph{Medical Image Analysis} ``Survey \& Critical Review'' track, \emph{NeurIPS Datasets and Benchmarks Track}, or \emph{Patterns}), or (b) deliver a positive contribution alongside the protocol.
  \item Naming three modules (DPE, MHRA, DFPN) and then disclaiming them in the same paper is a presentational choice that will confuse most reviewers. Pick a side: either the modules are part of a positive method, or they are not part of the paper.
\end{enumerate}

If these three issues are resolved, the reviewer would be enthusiastic about a resubmission. The current draft is one substantial revision away from a publishable evidence-protocol contribution.
\end{notebox}
